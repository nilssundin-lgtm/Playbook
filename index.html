    The Playbook

      What we believe.

      SPREEAI is building something that does not yet fully exist. That requires a team that moves with clarity, speed, and a shared sense of what matters. Our values define the posture we bring to that work: not just what we do, but how we think.

      Kind.
      We treat people well. Teammates, partners, customers. Kindness is not softness. It is the discipline of being honest and direct while still respecting the person across from you.
      Smart.
      We ask why before we act. We connect decisions to outcomes. We do not move fast toward the wrong thing and call it progress.
      Creative.
      We build things that do not yet fully exist. That requires genuine curiosity, the willingness to go outside your usual scope, and the belief that the right solution might look nothing like what came before it.
      Speed.
      We move. Responsiveness is a competitive advantage we cannot afford to lose. The cost of a wrong decision made quickly is almost always lower than the cost of a right decision made too late.

      These are not aspirations. They are the operating assumptions behind every decision we make.

      Great at SPREEAI.

      We are a small team operating at an outsized level. Everyone can own an outcome and a standard, without waiting to be managed. You bring the problem and the path forward. You pull in the right people early. You do not wait for someone to notice the gap. You close it.

      Put a bowtie on it.
      Starting is easy. Finishing is the job. Closing the loop, confirming it landed, making sure nothing is left hanging. The work is not done until it is actually done. This is how trust gets built here. Not in big moments, but in the quiet discipline of doing what you said you would do.
      Own your role. Cut through the noise. Finish the job.
      Own your lane.
      Know your function and be excellent at it. Become the person your teammates come to when something in your domain breaks or needs to get built. No job is beneath you. Your title describes what you lead, not what you are willing to do.
      Ownership also means knowing who owns what is not yours. When we are each clear on our lane and respectful of everyone else's, the whole team moves faster.
      Include your neighbors.
      Your neighbors are your cross-functional teammates. Anyone at SPREEAI whose work intersects with yours. In your own function, operate how you need to. But the moment your work crosses into another team's territory, bring people in. Not after the fact. Early, when it still matters and there is still time to shape the outcome.
      Inclusivity is not a value we hang on a wall. It is the habit of asking who else needs to be in this conversation before the decision gets made.
      Solve it fully.
      A bandaid fixes the symptom. It does not close the gap. Before you ship a patch, ask whether you are actually solving the problem or just buying time until it breaks again.
      If you would not put your name on it, it is not done. Slow down, solve it fully, then ship it.
      Win together.
      We win as a team or we do not win. We trust each other. We back each other. When something goes wrong, we do not look for who to blame. We look for what we missed and fix it together. Acknowledging failure as a team takes courage. It also takes ego off the table, and that is exactly where it belongs.
      Results matter. Individual credit does not.

      Communication.

      Good communication is not about volume. It is about precision. We say what needs to be said, to the right person, through the right channel, with enough context to act on it. Everything else is noise.

      Structure every message.
      Every message should answer four questions: what is this, who owns it, why does it matter, and what happens next. If it cannot answer all four, it is not ready to send. Lead with the ask. Context follows. If the message is long enough to require scrolling, put a TLDR at the top.

      TLDROne to two sentences. The full message in plain terms.
      WhatThe situation or request, stated directly.
      WhyThe business or customer impact. What breaks or improves.
      WhoClear ownership. One person is responsible, not the team.
      AskWhat do you need, and by when.
      Use the right channel.
      A message in the wrong channel creates noise for people who do not need it and buries it from the people who do. Ask yourself: who actually needs this? Send it there, not everywhere.

      Yes, no, and the reason behind both.
      Every decision deserves a reason. No without context is not a decision. It is a wall. This applies in every direction: to peers, to partners, to leadership.
      Escalate early.
      If something feels off, raise it. Do not wait. Escalation is not about blame. It is about protecting progress. Come prepared: state the problem, explain why it matters, name your recommendation, and say who needs to be involved. Escalating without context is noise. Escalating with it is leadership.
      Respect the team's time.
      Communicate during working hours. Sending messages outside of them implies an expectation of response. If something cannot wait, say so explicitly. Otherwise, schedule the message. When you are out, notify your supervisor, log it in Palmy, update your Google Calendar, and set your OOO. The team cannot absorb what you never communicated.

      Meetings.

      A meeting that could have been a Slack message is a tax on everyone in the room. We meet when it moves something forward. Before you schedule, ask: can this be a doc? Can it be a message? If yes, do that instead.

      Come prepared.
      No agenda, no meeting. Every invite goes out with a clear who, what, why, and ideal outcome. Who needs to be in the room? What are we here to decide or resolve? Why does it need to be a meeting? What does a good outcome look like? If you cannot answer all four, you are not ready to schedule. When a pre-read is attached, it is required preparation. Owners share it at least 24 hours before. Attendees come having read it.
      Meeting time is for discussion, not first reads.
      The right people in the room.
      Use required and optional in Google Calendar intentionally. Required means your presence and participation are essential. Optional means you benefit from knowing but do not need to be there. If someone does not need to speak or decide, make them optional. Send the notes afterward.
      Be on time. No late cancellations.
      Two-minute courtesy, then we start. Enable Speedy Meetings in Google Calendar. If you need to cancel, do it the evening before. A morning cancellation for a PST teammate who has planned their day around that time is not acceptable. The same applies when you are out for an extended period. Plan your absence the way you plan a meeting: with enough notice for the people around you to adjust.
      Respect Golden Hours.
      Golden Hours are protected focus time for the entire team, every day. Do not schedule meetings between 8am and 10am PST. No exceptions without explicit agreement from everyone you are inviting.

      Feedback.

      Feedback is not an annual event you brace for. It is a steady habit: small, frequent, and low-drama. We give it often, we give it with respect, and we make it specific enough to act on. In person or on a call for anything developmental or sensitive. Written for reinforcing patterns or following up. When in doubt, default to live.

      Direct.
      Bring feedback straight to the person it is for, and bring it early while there is still time to act on it. The receiver knows about it first. Always. Radical candor is honesty rooted in investment. We tell each other the truth directly because we care, and because we believe the person across from us can clear a higher bar.
      Actionable.
      Feedback that changes nothing was just conversation. Anchor every note in what you observed and where it leads: here is what I saw, here is the outcome it creates, here is what a stronger version looks like. Turn gaps into results with next steps. Every exchange ends with two questions answered: what is the feedback, and what is the action.
      Often.
      Give and recognize frequently. Positive and constructive in equal measure, because recognition is feedback too and it is the kind that compounds. Call out excellent work as readily as you flag a gap. When feedback flows constantly, no single note carries the weight of a verdict and everyone gets sharper.

      Handling tension.

      Tension is not a warning sign. It is proof that something matters. Every team building something real will feel friction. What defines us is not whether tension exists. It is how we show up when it does.

      Look inward first.
      Before you point anywhere else, point at yourself. Ask honestly: what is my part in this? Did I communicate clearly? Did I set the right expectations? Did I assume something I never confirmed? Owning your part is not about absorbing blame that is not yours. It is about being accountable enough to name what is.
      Find the why underneath the tension.
      Tension at the surface is rarely about what it looks like on the surface. Someone frustrated about a missed deadline might really be feeling like their priorities are invisible. Someone pushing back hard on a decision might feel cut out of a conversation that affected their work. Before you try to solve anything, slow down and ask what is actually going on. When both sides can name what they are trying to protect, the path forward gets a lot shorter.
      Assume good intent.
      Come to the conversation curious, not defensive. The default assumption at SPREEAI is that the person across from you is trying to do right by the team and the customer. They might be off on the approach. They might be missing context. But they are not the enemy. Ask questions before you make statements.
      Keep it small.
      Direct conversation first, always. When tension spreads before it is resolved, it pulls in people who do not have the full picture. Handle it at the lowest level possible. If the temperature is too high, take a break and schedule a follow-up within 24 hours. A pause only works with a hard end. When you come back, come back to resolve, not to relitigate.
      Resolve it. Write it down. Move on.
      Both people leave with a shared understanding of what was decided and what each person owns. Name the agreement. Make it clean. If it is not written down, it did not happen. When you cannot find common ground, come back to what the company and the customer need. That is not a tiebreaker. It is a reminder of why we are here.

      How we make decisions.

      Good decisions come from knowing why we are deciding, who owns the call, and where the truth is written down. Not every decision gets equal weight. Work is either above the line, moving us toward a client or revenue, or below it. When something moves above the line, something else moves below it. Track both. The decisions we defer are as important as the ones we make.

      Why before how.
      Before we scope it, staff it, or schedule the meeting, we name the problem. What are we actually solving? What happens if we do not? The how never gets to run ahead of the why.
      Revenue draws the line.
      Work is either above the line, meaning it moves us toward a client or revenue, or below it. Below the line is not worthless. It is just not what wins right now. When something moves above the line, name what moved below it. Track both. Deprioritization is a decision too, and it deserves the same clarity as the things we choose to do.
      Name one owner.
      Shared accountability is no accountability. Every cross-functional project has one Accountable owner who holds the call and the outcome. When you do not know who the owner is, that is the first decision to make.
      Write it down or it did not happen.
      A decision that lives only in someone's head is a future argument. Document what was decided, why, and what would make us revisit it. When memory and the doc disagree, the doc wins.

      Roadmaps and priorities.

      We do not just ship. We ship toward something. This section defines how we organize our work, how we move it forward, and what we expect from every function in making that happen.

      Start with the theme.
      A theme is a major strategic bet. It defines the direction we are committing to for the next six months. Themes are set by the Head of Product and agreed upon by the CEO. Goals and KPIs live beneath each theme, defining what success looks like and how we measure it. Every goal should connect to one of two outcomes: revenue growth or partner activation. If your work does not move one of those two needles, it is worth asking whether it belongs in the current cycle.
      Themes are not project lists. They are the strategic context that makes the project list make sense.
      Set priorities intentionally.
      Not everything that is possible is worth doing now. Before work gets resourced, it earns its place by passing three questions: Is it viable? Is it feasible? Is it desirable? Work that clears all three moves forward. Work that does not gets parked or cut.
      Work that does not gets parked or cut.
      P0 is a blocker.
      Nothing moves until it is resolved. P1 is critical to the current cycle with a defined owner, timeline, and deliverable. Everything else is sequenced behind them. Every function is expected to know where their work sits and to flag conflicts or blockers early, not after the deadline passes.
      Own your part of the plan.
      Every workstream has a clear owner and a clear timeline. We use RACIs to define who is Responsible, who is Accountable, who needs to be Consulted, and who needs to be Informed. When ownership is ambiguous, work stalls. Reliable execution means showing up to commitments. If a deadline is at risk, say so early. Surface the blocker, propose the adjustment, and keep the team informed. A delay that is communicated is manageable. A delay that is discovered is a problem.
      Move with intention. Finish what you start. Put a bowtie on it.
